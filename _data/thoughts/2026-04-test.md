---
title: Why freight never gets mentioned when we talk about transport poverty
date: 2026-04-03T22:33:11.336Z
category: Research
excerpt: Everyone talks about buses, metros, and whether poor neighbourhoods
  have access to jobs. Nobody asks whether the businesses in those
  neighbourhoods can actually receive goods, ship products, or stay connected to
  supply chains. That silence is what my PhD is about.
---
Transport poverty research has a blind spot. For decades, the conversation has focused almost entirely on people can they reach hospitals, schools, jobs? These are valid and important questions. But freight is invisible in this literature, and that invisibility has real consequences.

When a neighbourhood has poor road quality, unreliable logistics infrastructure, or no last-mile connectivity, it is not just residents who suffer. Local businesses face higher costs, longer lead times, and reduced competitiveness. Small manufacturers cannot scale. Markets fragment. Economic isolation deepens quietly, without anyone naming it as a transport problem.

My research tries to name it. The Freight Accessibility Index is an attempt to measure what has been systematically ignored: the degree to which a location is connected to freight networks, and what it costs when that connection breaks down.

Warsaw is my case study. Not because it is broken it is not but because even in a well-functioning city, the spatial variation in freight accessibility is striking once you start measuring it. Some corridors perform well. Others carry hidden penalties that compound over time.

The goal is not just academic. If we can measure freight accessibility the way we measure public transport accessibility, we can start designing policy around it. We can justify infrastructure investment in the right places, for the right reasons.

That is the work. It is slow, sometimes frustrating, and occasionally feels absurdly niche. But someone has to count the invisible things.

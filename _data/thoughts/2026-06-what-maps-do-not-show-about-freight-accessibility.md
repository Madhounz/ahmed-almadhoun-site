A map can show that two places are connected.

It can show a road, a route, a junction, or a corridor. But it cannot always show how well that connection actually works.

This is one of the main reasons why freight accessibility is more complex than it first appears.

For freight movement, the question is not only whether a route exists. The real question is whether that route performs well enough to support reliable and efficient logistics.

A road may connect an industrial area to an expressway, but if the surface condition is poor, the geometry is weak, travel times are unstable, or access is constrained, then the connection becomes less valuable in practice.

This kind of problem is often hidden.

The infrastructure is there, so on paper the area looks connected. But in reality, businesses may face higher delivery costs, longer travel times, lower reliability, and reduced competitiveness.

That is why freight accessibility needs to be measured through performance, not only distance.

In my research, I am interested in these hidden penalties. The small inefficiencies that do not always appear dramatic, but gradually affect how freight systems function.

Transport systems do not only fail when something collapses.

Sometimes they fail quietly.

They continue to operate, but with friction.

And for freight, that friction has a cost.

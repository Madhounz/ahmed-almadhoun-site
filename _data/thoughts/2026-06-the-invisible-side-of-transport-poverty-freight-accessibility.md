Most discussions about transport poverty focus on people: whether they can reach work, schools, hospitals, or public services.

That matters. But there is another side of transport poverty that is often ignored.

Freight.

Cities and regions do not only depend on people moving. They also depend on goods moving: food, medicine, construction materials, business supplies, spare parts, and the everyday logistics that keep the economy alive.

When freight access is weak, the impact is not always visible immediately. Roads may still exist. Deliveries may still happen. But costs rise, delays increase, reliability drops, and some areas become less attractive for business.

This is where my research begins.

I am interested in understanding how infrastructure quality, accessibility, and logistics performance shape economic connectivity. In simple terms: which places are well connected to freight systems, which places are disadvantaged, and how that disadvantage can be measured in a clear engineering way.

Freight transport poverty is not only about distance. It is also about road quality, travel time, reliability, service availability, and the hidden penalties that businesses face when infrastructure does not perform well.

My PhD work focuses on developing ways to identify and measure these problems, with Warsaw’s metropolitan freight network as one of the main case studies.

The goal is not only to describe the problem, but to create tools that can help planners, engineers, and decision-makers make better infrastructure choices.

Because sometimes the most important transport problems are not the ones people notice first.

They are the ones quietly increasing the cost of everything around them.
